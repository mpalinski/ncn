Research idea

  • Privacy management is a fundamental task of everyday online activity

    By selecting what data to disclose and to whom, people build and manage their online identities

  • User centric internet?

    We often do not feel in control of our data. We do not know its real value. As we start to recognize the financial value of our data, we could make different choices about what to share and with whom

  • Pay with your data

    What if we were able to get discounts for online services in exchange for our data? What would be our WTA?

  • Is it all about context?

    Different settings: P2P transportation & accomodation, GDPR mechanisms, mobile apps. Is personal data valued relatively or absolutely? Anchoring effects

Paper II

Welfare assessment of the GDPR

in collaboration with dr M. Sobolewski

published in Intereconomics and in the WNE WP series

Research idea

  • Model preferences over personal data protection tools with discrete choice experiment
  • Estimate willingness-to-pay for specific protection mechanisms
  • Estimate consumer surplus from the GDPR implementation
  • Explore preference heterogeneity with users’ attitudes towards privacy

Choice card

Main findings

  • Implementation of enhanced privacy control mechanisms will generate positive welfare effect.
  • Respondents assign substantial monetary value to the right to data erasure, objection to profiling and extended information duties of online services administrators.
  • Respondents largely underestimate the role of data portability.
  • Hybrid Choice Model: Privacy concerned respondents, female and respodents that experienced privacy violation in the past are less likely choose SQ and they have larger WTP for privacy enhacing mechnisms.

Paper III

Paying with your data

Privacy trade-offs on online platforms

planned submission: Computers in Human Behaviour

Research idea

  • Analyse users' preferences towards co-financing online services with personal data sharing and online activity with discrete choice experiment.
  • Test whether anchoring affects people’s elicited valuations for different types of personal data and digital activities
  • Estimate monetary valuation of privacy in the context of two types of app-mediated services: P2P transportation and P2P accommodation
  • Explore preference heterogeneity in users’ attitudes towards online privacy using HCM

Tested hypotheses

  • The monetary valuation people place on online privacy will vary across examined contexts: ride hailing and online vacation rental and will depend on the price of the service.
  • The monetary value people place on online privacy will vary depending on the type of private information or privacy sensitive actions being considered.
  • Exposure to a personal privacy breach negatively impacts a person’s willingness to accept the disclosure of private data and increases their monetary valuation of online privacy.
  • Privacy concern will explain heterogeneity of preferences towards sharing personal data and performing privacy sensitive actions.
  • Knowledge of privacy laws and regulation does have an impact on a person’s privacy preference increasing the monetary valuation of online privacy.
  • Increasing income has a positive impact on a person’s privacy preference and the valuation of online privacy.

Discrete choice experiment

  • Respondent makes choices from mutually exclusive sets of alternatives
  • In each choice alternatives are described by different levels of attributes (based on experimental design)
  • Respondents are asked to indicate the most preferred alternative
  • Changes in the stated choices enable derivation of the effects of the attributes on the choices, estimation of WTA remuneration for personal data and expected consumer surplus coming from privacy management options
  • HCM: extension of DCM aimed at including attitudinal variables

Main findings

  • There is a market for "pay with your data" business models
  • User's social media likes, name and e-mail address are the most sensitive personal data. Respondents are willing to perform privacy intrusive actions.
  • Only 6% of users chose status quo option
  • Personal data seems to be valued relatively to the cost of the service (anchoring effect)
